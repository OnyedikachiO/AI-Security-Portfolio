# Phase 1: AI Forensic Landscape

## Objective

Understand the purpose of AI forensics, how it differs from traditional digital forensics, and identify the types of evidence generated by AI systems.

---

## Background

Artificial Intelligence has become embedded in modern applications, enterprise environments, cloud services, and cybersecurity platforms. As these systems increasingly influence automated decision-making, investigators must understand how AI systems generate, process, and retain digital evidence.

Unlike traditional software, AI systems produce artifacts throughout multiple stages of operation, including model training, inference, prompt processing, retrieval pipelines, and system logging. These artifacts provide investigators with valuable evidence when analysing security incidents, model compromise, misuse, or malicious manipulation.

AI forensics extends conventional digital forensic methodologies by focusing on evidence generated by machine learning systems, large language models (LLMs), Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) pipelines, vector databases, prompts, embeddings, and model outputs.

Rather than examining only operating systems, applications, and network traffic, AI forensic investigations seek to determine:

- How an AI model generated a particular response.
- What data influenced the model's behaviour.
- Whether prompts or retrieved context manipulated the output.
- Whether training data or retrieved documents were compromised.
- Whether AI-generated decisions can be reconstructed during an investigation.

---

## AI Forensic Artifacts

AI systems generate numerous forensic artifacts that may be relevant during an investigation.

Examples include:

- Model files and checkpoints
- Training datasets
- Fine-tuning datasets
- Prompt history
- System prompts
- User prompts
- Model responses
- Conversation history
- Inference logs
- Retrieval logs
- Vector databases
- Embeddings
- API request logs
- API response logs
- Access logs
- Authentication records
- Audit logs
- Configuration files
- Plugin activity
- External tool interactions

These artifacts help investigators reconstruct AI behaviour, identify misuse, validate model outputs, and determine the sequence of events surrounding an incident.

---

## AI Forensics vs Traditional Digital Forensics

| Traditional Digital Forensics | AI Forensics |
|-------------------------------|--------------|
| Focuses on operating systems, endpoints, applications, and networks. | Focuses on AI models, prompts, embeddings, inference, retrieval systems, and AI-generated evidence. |
| Investigates user activity and system compromise. | Investigates model behaviour, prompt execution, retrieval processes, and AI decision-making. |
| Evidence includes files, logs, registry entries, memory, and network traffic. | Evidence includes prompts, model outputs, embeddings, vector stores, inference logs, and retrieval artifacts. |
| Reconstructs actions performed by users or malware. | Reconstructs how AI systems generated responses or reached decisions. |

---

## Key Takeaways

- AI forensics extends traditional digital forensics to AI-powered systems.
- AI investigations rely on artifacts generated throughout the AI lifecycle.
- Evidence may originate from prompts, embeddings, vector databases, model outputs, inference logs, and retrieval pipelines.
- Understanding AI-generated evidence is essential for investigating attacks against modern AI systems.

# Phase 2: AI Limitations

## Objective

Understand the inherent limitations of AI systems and recognize why these limitations present challenges during forensic investigations.

---

## Background

Although Artificial Intelligence can process vast amounts of information, identify patterns, and automate analysis, it is not capable of understanding context in the same way as a human investigator. AI models generate outputs based on learned statistical relationships rather than reasoning, intent, or true comprehension.

These limitations become significant during forensic investigations, where evidence must be interpreted accurately, preserved correctly, and validated before conclusions can be drawn.

---

## Hallucinations

AI models may generate responses that appear accurate and convincing but are factually incorrect. These hallucinations can include fabricated events, nonexistent references, incorrect technical details, or false explanations.

Because generated responses may appear highly confident, investigators must independently verify all AI-generated findings before relying on them during an investigation.

---

## Lack of Explainability

Many AI models operate as black-box systems. While they can produce accurate outputs, the internal reasoning process that produced those outputs is often not transparent.

This makes it difficult to determine:

- Why a particular response was generated.
- Which data most influenced the decision.
- Whether the response was affected by training data or retrieved context.
- Whether manipulation occurred during inference.

The inability to fully explain model behaviour complicates forensic investigations.

---

## Bias

AI models inherit characteristics from the data used during training.

If training data contains bias, incomplete information, or inaccurate representations, those characteristics may influence future model outputs.

Investigators should consider the possibility that AI-generated results may reflect underlying dataset bias rather than objective conclusions.

---

## Dependence on Training Data

The quality of an AI model depends directly on the quality of its training data.

Incomplete, outdated, manipulated, or poisoned datasets may influence model behaviour long after training has completed.

As a result, investigators should consider whether abnormal behaviour originates from:

- Training data
- Fine-tuning data
- Retrieved external knowledge
- Prompt manipulation

---

## Limited Context

AI systems operate only on the information available within their context window.

If relevant information is unavailable or omitted, the model may generate incomplete or inaccurate responses.

Similarly, large context windows may introduce unrelated information that influences generated outputs.

---

## Security Implications

These limitations affect forensic investigations because they can:

- Produce inaccurate findings.
- Complicate evidence validation.
- Obscure the origin of generated responses.
- Make incident reconstruction more difficult.
- Increase the likelihood of incorrect conclusions if AI output is accepted without verification.

---

## Key Takeaways

- AI-generated output should never be treated as authoritative evidence without validation.
- Hallucinations can introduce false information into investigations.
- Black-box model behaviour limits explainability.
- Bias and training data quality directly influence model behaviour.
- AI should support forensic investigations, not replace human analysis and verification.

# Phase 3: AI and Digital Forensics (DFIR)

## Objective

Understand how Artificial Intelligence supports Digital Forensics and Incident Response (DFIR), while recognising its role as an investigative aid rather than a replacement for human expertise.

---

## Background

Digital Forensics and Incident Response (DFIR) involves identifying, preserving, analysing, and presenting digital evidence following a security incident. As organisations generate increasingly large volumes of logs, alerts, network traffic, and endpoint telemetry, AI has become an important capability for assisting investigators in processing this information more efficiently.

Rather than replacing forensic analysts, AI helps automate repetitive tasks, identify patterns across large datasets, accelerate evidence review, and support incident investigations.

---

## AI in Digital Forensics

AI can assist forensic investigations by processing large volumes of structured and unstructured data significantly faster than manual analysis.

Common applications include:

- Log analysis
- Malware classification
- Network traffic analysis
- Threat detection
- Incident correlation
- Timeline reconstruction
- Evidence prioritisation
- Threat intelligence analysis

These capabilities allow investigators to reduce manual effort while focusing on validating findings and making investigative decisions.

---

## AI in Incident Response

During incident response, AI can assist security teams by identifying suspicious behaviour, correlating security events, and highlighting indicators that may require immediate investigation.

Examples include:

- Detecting anomalous user behaviour
- Identifying unusual authentication activity
- Correlating alerts from multiple security tools
- Prioritising high-risk events
- Assisting with threat hunting
- Supporting malware investigations

These functions improve operational efficiency but still require human verification before response actions are taken.

---

## AI-Assisted Evidence Analysis

AI can rapidly analyse forensic artifacts collected during an investigation, including:

- System logs
- Authentication records
- Network traffic
- Endpoint telemetry
- Process execution logs
- File metadata
- Audit records
- Security alerts

Pattern recognition enables investigators to identify relationships that might otherwise be overlooked during manual analysis.

---

## Human Validation

Despite its analytical capabilities, AI cannot independently determine the accuracy or legal admissibility of digital evidence.

Investigators remain responsible for:

- Validating AI-generated findings
- Confirming evidence authenticity
- Preserving chain of custody
- Correlating evidence from multiple sources
- Making investigative conclusions

AI should support forensic decision-making rather than replace professional judgement.

---

## Security Considerations

When incorporating AI into DFIR workflows, investigators should consider:

- Hallucinated or inaccurate outputs
- Bias introduced by training data
- Lack of explainability
- Data privacy concerns
- Integrity of AI-generated evidence
- Model limitations during investigations

These factors reinforce the need for independent verification throughout the forensic process.

---

## Key Takeaways

- AI enhances Digital Forensics and Incident Response by accelerating evidence analysis.
- AI assists with log analysis, threat detection, malware analysis, and incident correlation.
- Human investigators remain responsible for validating AI-generated findings.
- AI should be treated as an investigative support capability rather than an authoritative source of forensic evidence.

# Phase 4: Legal and Ethical Implications

## Objective

Understand the legal, ethical, and governance considerations involved in AI forensic investigations and the handling of AI-generated evidence.

---

## Background

As Artificial Intelligence becomes increasingly integrated into business operations, critical infrastructure, healthcare, finance, and cybersecurity, investigators must consider not only the technical aspects of AI evidence but also its legal and ethical implications.

Unlike traditional digital evidence, AI-generated outputs may be influenced by opaque decision-making processes, training data, prompt context, and retrieval mechanisms. These characteristics introduce new challenges when determining the reliability, admissibility, and accountability of AI-generated evidence.

---

## Evidence Integrity

Digital evidence must remain complete, accurate, and unaltered throughout an investigation.

When AI systems participate in evidence collection or analysis, investigators must ensure that:

- Original evidence remains unchanged.
- AI-generated outputs are distinguishable from original evidence.
- Analysis can be reproduced where possible.
- AI-generated conclusions are independently validated.

Maintaining evidence integrity remains essential throughout the investigative process.

---

## Chain of Custody

The chain of custody documents how evidence is collected, preserved, transferred, analysed, and presented.

When AI systems are used during forensic investigations, investigators should document:

- The AI system used.
- Model version.
- Configuration settings.
- Input data provided.
- Generated outputs.
- Human validation performed.

Proper documentation supports transparency and preserves the integrity of the investigation.

---

## Privacy Considerations

AI investigations frequently involve sensitive information, including:

- Personally Identifiable Information (PII)
- Organisational data
- Customer records
- Internal communications
- Security logs
- Incident reports

Investigators should ensure that AI-assisted forensic analysis complies with applicable privacy requirements and organisational data protection policies.

---

## Accountability

AI systems assist investigators but do not assume responsibility for investigative decisions.

Human investigators remain accountable for:

- Evidence collection
- Evidence interpretation
- Validation of AI-generated findings
- Incident conclusions
- Reporting
- Legal testimony

Responsibility for investigative outcomes cannot be delegated to AI systems.

---

## Ethical Considerations

The use of AI during forensic investigations should be guided by ethical principles that promote:

- Fairness
- Transparency
- Accountability
- Privacy
- Responsible handling of digital evidence

Investigators should recognise the limitations of AI and avoid treating generated outputs as unquestionable facts.

---

## Governance

Organisations adopting AI-assisted forensic capabilities should establish governance processes that define:

- Acceptable use of AI during investigations
- Evidence handling procedures
- Validation requirements
- Documentation standards
- Access controls
- Audit processes

Governance ensures AI technologies are used responsibly while supporting defensible forensic investigations.

---

## Key Takeaways

- AI-generated evidence requires careful validation before being relied upon during investigations.
- Evidence integrity and chain of custody remain fundamental principles of digital forensics.
- Human investigators retain responsibility for investigative decisions.
- Legal, ethical, and governance considerations are essential when incorporating AI into forensic workflows.

# Phase 4: AI Forensic Investigation Workflow

## Objective

Develop a structured methodology for investigating security incidents involving AI systems by preserving evidence, identifying attack vectors, analysing model behaviour, and documenting findings.

---

## Why AI Forensics Is Different

Traditional digital forensics focuses on systems, files, and network activity. AI forensics expands the investigation scope by including:

- AI models
- Training datasets
- Prompt histories
- Retrieval pipelines
- Vector databases
- Model outputs
- System logs
- Configuration files

Investigators must determine not only **what happened**, but also **why the AI system produced a particular behaviour**.

---

# AI Forensic Investigation Process

A complete AI forensic investigation generally follows several phases.

## 1. Incident Identification

The investigation begins after suspicious AI behaviour is detected.

Examples include:

- Sensitive information disclosure
- Unexpected model outputs
- Hallucinated responses
- Prompt injection attacks
- Data poisoning
- Retrieval manipulation
- Unauthorized tool execution
- Suspicious model behaviour

The objective is to determine whether the behaviour resulted from:

- User interaction
- Model compromise
- Dataset compromise
- Infrastructure compromise
- Configuration errors

---

## 2. Evidence Preservation

Before making any changes, investigators preserve evidence.

Potential evidence includes:

- Prompt history
- Model responses
- System prompts
- Conversation history
- API requests
- API responses
- Retrieval logs
- Vector database snapshots
- Model versions
- Configuration files
- Access logs
- Authentication records
- Network traffic
- Container images
- Memory captures

Evidence integrity must be maintained throughout the investigation.

---

## 3. Evidence Collection

Evidence should be collected from every component involved in the AI pipeline.

Common evidence sources include:

### User Layer

- User prompts
- Conversation history
- User identity
- Session information

### Application Layer

- API logs
- Authentication logs
- Request metadata
- Rate limiting events

### AI Layer

- System prompts
- Model responses
- Temperature settings
- Model version
- Safety configurations

### Retrieval Layer

- Retrieved document chunks
- Similarity scores
- Top-k values
- Metadata filters
- Namespace information

### Vector Database

- Stored embeddings
- Metadata
- Collection configuration
- Access policies

### Infrastructure Layer

- Server logs
- Container logs
- Kubernetes logs
- Cloud audit logs
- Network telemetry

---

## 4. Timeline Reconstruction

Investigators reconstruct the complete sequence of events.

Typical timeline components include:

1. User submits prompt.
2. Authentication occurs.
3. Prompt reaches the application.
4. Embedding is generated.
5. Similarity search executes.
6. Documents are retrieved.
7. Prompt is augmented.
8. Model generates output.
9. Response returned to user.
10. Logs written.

A timeline helps identify exactly where compromise occurred.

---

# Root Cause Analysis

The objective is to identify **why** the incident occurred rather than simply documenting the observable symptoms.

Possible causes include:

- Prompt injection
- Data poisoning
- Overly broad retrieval
- Weak metadata filtering
- Missing access control
- Model misconfiguration
- Unsafe plugins
- Tool abuse
- Logging exposure
- Training data contamination

Root cause analysis focuses on the originating failure rather than the resulting behaviour.

---

# AI-Specific Evidence

Unlike traditional investigations, AI incidents require analysing additional evidence unique to AI systems.

Examples include:

- Prompt templates

- Conversation context
- Retrieval context
- Retrieved documents
- Embeddings

# Chain of Custody

Evidence should remain traceable throughout the investigation.

Chain of custody records typically include:

- Evidence identifier

- Collector
- Storage location
- Integrity verification
- Access history

Maintaining chain of custody supports evidence integrity and investigation reliability.

---

# Investigation Documentation

The final investigation should document:

- Incident summary
- Scope
- Timeline
- Evidence collected
- Root cause
- Impact assessment
- Containment actions
- Recovery actions
- Lessons learned
- Security recommendations

Documentation enables future investigations and supports continuous security improvement.

- Vector databases may contain evidence of poisoning or retrieval failures.

# Phase 5: AI Memory Forensics

## Objective

Investigate how AI systems retain conversational context, identify artefacts left behind during interactions, and understand the forensic value of conversation memory.

---

## Background

Unlike traditional software, AI assistants maintain context throughout conversations. This contextual memory enables coherent multi-turn interactions but also introduces forensic considerations.

Conversation history, cached prompts, retrieved context, and session state can become valuable evidence during security investigations. Analysts must understand what information persists, how it is stored, and how it influences future responses.

Memory forensics focuses on identifying these artefacts while determining whether sensitive information remains accessible beyond its intended lifetime.

---

## Investigation

The investigation examined how conversational memory persists across multiple interactions.

The assessment focused on identifying:

- Session memory
- Context persistence
- Prompt history
- Retrieved context
- Residual conversation artefacts

The exercise demonstrated that AI systems continually build contextual state throughout an interaction rather than treating every request independently.

---

## Evidence

Conversation context remained available throughout the investigation, allowing previous prompts to influence subsequent responses.

Observed artefacts included:

- Previous user prompts
- Assistant responses
- Conversation history
- Retrieved contextual information
- Session state

---

## Technical Analysis

AI conversational memory differs significantly from conventional application memory.

Instead of storing executable state, AI systems preserve contextual information that guides future inference.

Potential forensic artefacts include:

- Prompt history
- Retrieved documents
- System prompts
- User instructions
- Conversation summaries
- Context windows
- Session metadata

During investigations, these artefacts help reconstruct:

- User activity
- AI decision flow
- Prompt evolution
- Retrieval behaviour
- Sensitive information exposure

---

## Security Considerations

Persistent conversation memory introduces several risks:

- Disclosure of previous prompts
- Exposure of sensitive contextual information
- Leakage of retrieved documents
- Unintended prompt inheritance
- Cross-session data persistence

Proper memory lifecycle management reduces the likelihood of sensitive information remaining accessible longer than necessary.

A structured investigation process ensures evidence integrity while allowing investigators to accurately determine how an AI system behaved during an incident.

- Collecting relevant forensic artefacts
- Preserving evidence integrity
- Analysing prompts and responses
- Reviewing model behaviour

Investigators evaluate:

- Input prompts
- Context windows

- Configuration settings

## Security Considerations

- Evidence preservation
- Chain of custody
- Log integrity

- Correlation across AI and traditional infrastructure

# Phase 7: Reporting and Lessons Learned

Document forensic findings, communicate investigation results, and identify security improvements that reduce the likelihood of future AI-related incidents.

---

## Background

The final stage of an AI forensic investigation focuses on translating technical findings into actionable recommendations.

Effective reporting ensures that investigators, security teams, management, and stakeholders understand what occurred, why it happened, and what controls should be implemented to prevent similar incidents.

A well-structured forensic report also preserves evidence for future audits, regulatory reviews, and incident response activities.

---

## Investigation

Following evidence collection and analysis, the investigation documented:

- Timeline of events
- Evidence collected
- Observed AI behaviour
- Root cause analysis
- Security weaknesses
- Impact assessment
- Recommended mitigations

The report consolidated findings from prompts, model responses, retrieval processes, logs, and supporting infrastructure.

The investigation report incorporated evidence gathered throughout the forensic process, including:

- Conversation history
- Prompt sequences
- AI-generated responses
- Retrieval records
- System logs

## Technical Analysis

The investigation demonstrated that AI incidents rarely result from a single failure.

Instead, incidents often arise through a combination of weaknesses affecting:

- Data handling
- Prompt processing
- Retrieval mechanisms
- Logging practices
- Access controls

- Protect forensic artefacts from modification
- Restrict access to sensitive AI data
- Monitor AI system activity continuously
- Review retrieval and logging configurations
- Maintain secure evidence handling procedures
- Document investigation findings thoroughly

These controls strengthen both incident response capabilities and long-term AI governance.
